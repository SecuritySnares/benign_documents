Background:  The concept of a ‘deliverable’ is fundamental to software development activities.  Deliverables represent the status of a project to a particular point in time.  Some deliverables are internal to the development / management team, while others are quite specific and presented to the customer.

 

I view the deliverables below as a requirement for each team to submit a professional product meeting that meets the enumerated requirements of the specific deliverable.  These are to appear professional in every respect – formatting, grammar, presentation, etc.  Lack of professionalism in your deliverable is strongly discouraged. 

 

At the end of each deliverable, there is to be a page with each team member’s signature attesting to the fact that they ‘buy into’ the deliverable.  This is your chance to review the document for compliance with the deliverable specifications along with formatting, spelling, etc. requirements.  Your signature (to me) means that you have viewed the deliverable and are satisfied with its contents.  The grade earned by the deliverable will then be applied to each team member’s individual grade.

 

Please note that it has been my experience that team members often ‘cut and paste’ their deliverables together – not really attempting to ensure the entire document is well-presented.  I have frequently found misspellings, poor formatting, missing items, etc. commonplace.  This is unacceptable work for a deliverable.  So, please take pride in your work and ensure your deliverables represent your very best effort.  Do it right the first time!

            Use Cases:  For the Filled Use Cases, use Appendix B in the Use Cases

text as your guide.  (Note the ‘flow’ is abstract and implementation independent.)

 

In the Alternative and Exception parts of the Use Case, you are only to identify (list) the Alternative Flows and the Exception Flows.  For example, if an alternative flows would start at steps 3, 6, and 7 of the Basic Flow of Events, then in the Alternative Flows, you should have an annotation:  A3.  <descriptor>.  Then (new line), A6.  <descriptor>, etc. 

 

Example:

A3.  Card cannot be identified

A6.  Nonstandard amount required

A7.  The ATM is out of money.

(for Exception Paths, use “E” and a reference to the Step number in the Basic Flow of Events.)

 

The Traceability Indicators entry is to have the Use Case number, like 4, followed by the appropriate alternative / exception indicator.  So, there will be entries like:  4A2;   4A4;  4E2.

 

Scenarios:  For the basic (happy) path, you are to supply a scenario.  Use page 46 in the same text as your model – for the Scenario portion.  Include test data. 

 

You need to include at least a couple of other scenarios in your deliverable – at least one alternative flow and at least one exception flow.  This need not be burdensome.  I would suggest describing an alternative flow that you think is significant, as well as an exception flow that you similarly feel is significant.

 

Each scenario should be numbered, as in 4 or 4A2, etc. and include the words Scenario or Alternate Scenario or Exception Scenario in the left column where appropriate.  The name of the Use Case will still be in the template above.  So, the user will be able to tell if this is a basic scenario, alternative, or exception scenario and to which Use Case it is linked.

 

Recognize that scenarios are ‘instantiations’ of use cases, whether they are basic flows of events, alternative use cases, or exception use cases.  As such, they should have discrete values in the steps as much as practical at this stage. Without values, they will look just like the Use Cases, and will not be of much help.  With values, these scenarios will form the basis for testing later.  So, do add values.
