How paradigms are created

Kuhn, Thomas, S.: The structure of Scientific Revolutions, Second edition, Chicago, 1970, (1962), Chapter2, p. 16-19.

According to Kuhn a paradigm is created when more than mere facts are at hand within a scientific inquiry. Which appears, according to Kuhn (1970), during these early stages of inquiry, when different researchers confronting the same phenomena, describing and interpreting them in different ways. Kuhn (1970) then points out that what is surprising, and perhaps also unique in its degree to the fields we call science, is that such initial divergences should ever largely disappear. For they do disappear, according to Kuhn (1970), to very considerable extent and then apparently once and for all. Kuhn (1970) argues furthermore that their disappearance is usually caused by the triumph of one of the pre-paradigm schools, which, because of its own characteristic beliefs and preconceptions, emphasized only some special part of the too sizable and inchoate pool of information. So then, from the competition of these pre-paradigmatic schools, one paradigm emerges.Kuhn (1970) points out that to be accepted as a paradigm, a theory must seem better than its competitions, but it need not, and in fact ever does, explain all the facts with which it can be confronted. According to Kuhn (1970) when an individual or group first produces a synthesis able to attract most of the next generation’s practitioners the older schools gradually disappears. In their disappear is caused by their member’s convention to the new paradigm. Kuhn (1970) point out a pretty funny thing here, that could be compared with the system engineering community, there are always some people who cling to one or another of the older views, and the are simply read out of the profession, which thereafter ignores their work. The new paradigm implies a new and more rigid definition of the field. Those unwilling or unable to accommodate their work to it must proceed in isolation or attach themselves to some other group. According to Kuhn (1970) they historically have often simply stayed in the department of philosophy from which so many of the special science have been spawned.
